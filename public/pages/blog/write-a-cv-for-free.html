How to write a clean CV for free — a practical step-by-step

April 18, 2026 · 6 min read

A CV is not a biography — it's a focused, one-page pitch. Here's a short guide to writing one that's readable, relevant, and ready to send, without paying for a builder or signing up for anything.

Step 1: pick a structure

Most modern CVs follow a chronological structure:

  1. Header — your name, role, and contact information.
  2. Summary — a 2–3 sentence snapshot of who you are.
  3. Experience — most recent first, with measurable impact.
  4. Education — degree, institution, year.
  5. Skills — short list, relevant to the role.

Step 2: write impact-focused bullets

Hiring managers skim. Each experience bullet should answer: what did you do, and what changed? Weak: "Responsible for customer support." Strong: "Handled 60 tickets/week, reducing average response time from 12h to 3h."

Step 3: keep it short

For most people under 10 years of experience, one page is ideal. Two pages maximum. Remove anything older than ~10 years unless it's uniquely relevant. Remove hobbies unless they genuinely differentiate you.

Step 4: choose a template

Use a template that's ATS-friendly (parseable by automated systems). Avoid heavy graphics, two-column layouts with unusual fonts, and decorative icons. Clean typography and generous spacing beat "creative" layouts for most roles.

Step 5: tailor for the role

Read the job posting. Note the 3–5 most emphasized skills. Make sure those appear in your summary and recent experience. Don't invent experience — reframe what you've done using the same vocabulary.

Step 6: proofread, then export

Read it aloud — typos hide in silent reading. Check your contact information is correct. Save as PDF (not .docx) to preserve layout across devices.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Photos — usually a negative in English-speaking markets.
  • Personal pronouns — drop the "I" in bullets.
  • Objective statements — replaced by summaries since ~2015.
  • References available on request — implied and unnecessary.
  • Dense paragraphs — hiring managers scan bullets, not prose.
